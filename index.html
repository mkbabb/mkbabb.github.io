E-Rate optimization
A variant of a prototypical knapsack problem [1]: given an arbitrary knapsack that can hold N units of items, what is the largest number of items, a0, a1 ... an, weighing each w0, w1 ... wn, one can fit into said knapsack not exceeding N units. Our problem: given n buckets (in our case three for 2019), what schools should be placed into said buckets such that the total discount is maximized. An effective algorithm was developed to solve just this problem. More work to follow.

The algorithm
The overarching algorithm is Monte Carlo based, employing a genetic variation within. Concretely, it may described as thus: randomly select a set of schools, place them in a bucket, and look at the cost savings. The backbone of the method is dependent of and architectured upon the concept of randomness, or at least pseudo-randomness. The random number generator heretofore used is based off of the latest innovations in, well, random number generators [1].
  1. Said research can be found here. Thanks, Melissa!
Delineation
We endearingly denote each batch of all ~250 schools as a 'critter': with each having an associated fitness measure, or cost savings. A set of critters is randomly spawned forth; set to compete with each other. At then end of a critter's life, its fitness measure is thereupon analyzed, and used to determine the 'mating' procedure. The 'genes' from each critter are then interlarded within another set of randomly generated 'children', thus beginning another life; the circle of life.

In our instance, the fitness measure is the total cost savings, and each gene is an individual school with its corresponding bucket tag [1].

The results. Expanded.
The hereinbefore mentioned $500,000 savings: how and what? Utilizing the above heuristics, we were able to initialize a series of test runs, each spawned forth with varying 'hyper-parameters' [1] and running for several hours. The results speak for themselves: with 3 buckets we reached a cost savings of over $400,000 in a few hours. With four, a cost savings of over $500,000 in a day [2]. The implementation process is decidedly straight forward: put the set of schools output by the program into the pre-stipulated buckets. Done. The paper-work on the other hand... [3]

The Analysis.
What did we do with it? It's an ongoing process, but the hitherto implemented analyses are: correlations of each column of desideratum within the data set, as well as granular analysis of several pertinent questions, such as reliable internet, cleanliness and overall satisfaction.

Some insights gathered: as a sanity check, some of the working conditions columns quite intuitively correlate with others: for example, there's a high correlation value between the questions of requiring internet usage for homework and requiring an internet enabled device for homework (triviality notwithstanding, this is useful!).
